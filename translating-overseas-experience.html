Translating overseas experience

Many international candidates are filtered out not because they lack ability, but because their experience is described in a way the employer does not yet know how to read. This page focuses on translation rather than suspicion.

Interpreting unfamiliar titles

Job titles do not transfer neatly between countries. One title may imply a different level of seniority, team size or scope elsewhere. Employers often get a better picture when they read beyond the title and look at the work done, decisions made and outcomes achieved.

Recognising transferable skills

Project delivery, stakeholder management, technical analysis, supervision, systems improvement and client-facing communication can all transfer across systems even when the surrounding institutions differ. The question is usually how to map the skill, not whether the skill exists.

Looking beyond local terminology

Local terminology can create false confidence in some CVs and false doubt in others. A more useful approach is to ask what the person was responsible for, how complexity showed up in the role, and what evidence suggests they can adapt to your setting.

Practical examples

A “project coordinator” in one market may have done work closer to a UK project officer or PMO analyst. A hospital role abroad may carry greater autonomy than its nearest title equivalent suggests. Translation improves when employers ask what the role required in practice.
