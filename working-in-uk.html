Working in the UK

This page explains the main structure of working life in the UK. It is designed for orientation first, so you can understand how the labour market works before making detailed decisions.

How the UK labour market works

The UK labour market is made up of public, private and voluntary sector employers. Some recruit internationally often, while others mainly hire people who are already in the UK. Knowing that difference can save time and reduce unnecessary rejections.

Most jobs are filled through direct applications, recruiters, referrals or public vacancy platforms. Different sectors rely on these routes in different ways.

Common employment types

Common contract types include full-time, part-time, fixed-term, temporary, agency and self-employed work. These labels matter because they affect job security, holiday pay, sick pay and how rights apply in practice.

Do not assume every paid role works the same way. It helps to check what type of status the employer is offering before accepting it.

Sponsorship basics

For the Skilled Worker route, a person must work for a UK employer approved by the Home Office and have a certificate of sponsorship for an eligible role. GOV.UK also publishes the register of licensed sponsors, which can help people identify employers already set up to sponsor workers.

What employers often expect

Many employers look for more than qualifications. They often want clear communication, evidence of outcomes, understanding of the role, and signs that someone can work in the way that sector normally works in the UK.

Sometimes the real barrier is not skill level. It is unfamiliarity with UK job titles, application habits or professional language.

London versus other UK cities

London has strong concentration in business services, finance, technology, life sciences, culture and the visitor economy. It offers visibility and networks, but it also brings higher costs and more competition.

London can be the right target when your sector has real depth there. It should be a strategic choice, not an automatic one.
